Semisupervised Mean Inference For Applied ML

Calibrated AIPW that feels like a package, not a research prototype.

ppi_aipw gives you a practical workflow for semisupervised mean estimation when you have labeled outcomes, a larger unlabeled pool, and predictions from a fixed model. It pairs AIPW with post-hoc calibration and lets you choose between fast Wald intervals and refit-everything bootstrap intervals.

Quickstart

What the arguments mean

The main API is intentionally small. You provide labeled outcomes, labeled predictions, unlabeled predictions, and your choices for calibration and interval construction.

Y

Observed outcomes for the labeled sample.

Yhat

Predictions on the same labeled rows.

Yhat_unlabeled

Predictions on the unlabeled sample.

method

Choose "aipw", "linear", "platt", or "isocal".

inference

Choose "wald" for speed or "bootstrap" for a refit-everything interval.

w, w_unlabeled

Optional observation weights for labeled and unlabeled samples.

      Historical Review

      The PPI story is bigger than the recent branding

      This section is based on the historical framing in your paper. The key message is that PPI sits inside a longer semiparametric and missing-data tradition, and that calibration gives a clean way to connect prediction-powered practice back to AIPW and debiased ML.

      1970s-2000s

      Missing data and semiparametric efficiency came first

      Long before “prediction-powered inference,” semiparametric statistics developed influence-function methods, doubly robust estimators, and AIPW-style procedures for missing-at-random outcomes. In your paper’s framing, the semisupervised mean problem is one instance of that older missing-outcome setup.

      1990s-2020s

      Debiased ML made black-box prediction compatible with valid inference

      Debiased machine learning, TMLE, and related orthogonal methods showed how to combine flexible prediction models with valid inference. That matters here because semisupervised mean estimation does not require inventing a new inferential logic: the AIPW logic already exists.

      Recent ML framing

      PPI popularized the setup for modern black-box predictors

      Recent ML papers recast the same broad problem as prediction-powered inference: small labeled sample, large unlabeled sample, and a useful but imperfect predictor. Your paper argues that this reframing is useful operationally, but the estimators themselves should still be understood through the older AIPW and efficiency lens.

      This paper’s synthesis

      Calibration clarifies what PPI is doing and what can improve it

      Your paper’s central historical claim is that PPI and PPI++ are special cases of AIPW, with PPI++ coinciding algebraically with linear calibration in the one-score setting. That opens the door to richer calibrators like isotonic regression, which keep the simple “predict, calibrate, average” workflow while improving efficiency when miscalibration is genuinely nonlinear.

      Why this matters for practitioners

      You do not need to choose between a modern ML workflow and classical inferential guarantees. The website’s core design follows the paper’s perspective: start from the familiar AIPW baseline, use calibration to improve the score when it is mis-scaled, and interpret PPI-style estimators as part of that broader family rather than as an isolated new paradigm.

      Empirical Takeaways

      What the paper and package checks suggest in practice

      Default workflow

      Start with linear calibration and Wald intervals.

      Binary or bounded scores

      Platt is a sensible robustness check when a smooth sigmoid recalibration is plausible.

      Nonlinear miscalibration

      IsoCal is attractive when monotonicity is believable and labeled data are large enough to fit a stable curve.

      Where PPI fits

      Use AIPW as the baseline score-based estimator; vanilla PPI is not the natural default comparator once pooled-score information is available.

      Where this website’s framing comes from

      The historical review and practitioner recommendations on this page are drawn from the paper text in main.tex and ppi_reproduction_experiments.tex in this repository.

      Main paper themes reflected here:

      • PPI and PPI++ can be understood within the older AIPW and semiparametric-efficiency framework.
      • Calibration is stronger than mean-bias correction alone and can improve semisupervised inference when the score is miscalibrated.
      • Linear calibration is a strong practical default, while isotonic calibration is a useful nonlinear upgrade when labeled data support it.
      • Bootstrap intervals are a practical complement to Wald intervals.
