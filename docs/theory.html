Theory

How the estimator works in simple terms.

In the small-labeled, large-unlabeled setting, predictions can improve precision, but they should not be trusted blindly. AIPW uses those predictions and then adds a labeled-sample correction, so you get the efficiency benefit of a good score together with a built-in safeguard against prediction error.

Core Recipe

Use predictions Average a score over labeled and unlabeled covariates
Correct with labels Add back the average residual

The score drives efficiency. The labeled correction keeps the estimator anchored to observed outcomes.

Main Idea

AIPW is not one special formula with one special prediction model. It is a broad template, and you are free to choose the score \(f(X)\) that enters it.

Estimator

The estimator in one line

One part averages a score over labeled and unlabeled covariates. The other part corrects it using the rows where outcomes are observed.

estimate = pooled average of a score + average labeled correction

The score term uses all the covariate information you have. The correction term measures how far that score misses the truth on labeled rows and adds the discrepancy back. That is why the estimator stays approximately unbiased even when predictions are imperfect.

Notation

Let the labeled sample be \((X_1, Y_1), \ldots, (X_n, Y_n)\) and the unlabeled sample be \(\widetilde X_1, \ldots, \widetilde X_N\). Here \(f(X)\) is the score we choose to plug into AIPW.

AIPW for a chosen score

A simple semisupervised AIPW estimator for the mean can be written as:

\[ \hat\theta(f) = \frac{1}{n+N} \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(X_i) + \sum_{j=1}^{N} f(\widetilde X_j) \right\} + \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\{Y_i-f(X_i)\}. \]

Why this form matters

The first part is a pooled plug-in average of the score over labeled and unlabeled covariates. The second part is the labeled residual correction. Many familiar semisupervised estimators are just special cases of this template.

A good score helps the pooled plug-in term. The correction term keeps the estimate tied to observed outcomes.

AIPW Class

AIPW is a class, not one estimator

The score choice controls efficiency.

You choose the score

The score \(f(X)\) can be a raw prediction, a linear recalibration, a monotone calibration map, or another data-adaptive score. AIPW supplies the correction template around it.

Better scores improve efficiency

If the score is closer to the true outcome regression \(\mu_0(X)=E_0[Y \mid X]\), then the residual correction is smaller and the final estimator is typically more efficient.

Efficiency is the practical goal

In practice, this means tighter intervals. The best score is the one that predicts the outcome level well, not just the ranking.

Efficiency Target

At the population level, the most efficient score is the one that makes the large-sample variance of the AIPW estimator as small as possible. Up to a centering constant, that means choosing \(f\) to make \(E_0[(Y-f(X))^2]\) small.

In practice, that means we can search over scaled scores, linear recalibrations, spline or isotonic calibrators, or richer machine-learning regression classes.

PPI and PPI++

Where PPI and PPI++ fit

These are particular choices inside the broader AIPW family, not unrelated estimators.

Standard AIPW

With the raw score \(m(X)\), standard AIPW uses the pooled plug-in average:

\[ \hat\theta_{\mathrm{AIPW}} = \frac{1}{n+N} \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} m(X_i) + \sum_{j=1}^{N} m(\widetilde X_j) \right\} + \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\{Y_i-m(X_i)\}. \]

It uses the same correction term as PPI, but keeps the labeled covariates in the plug-in average.

PPI

If \(m(X)\) is the raw prediction score, then plain PPI uses the raw score in the unlabeled-only plug-in form:

\[ \hat\theta_{\mathrm{PPI}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N} m(\widetilde X_j) + \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\{Y_i-m(X_i)\}. \]

Within the common AIPW family, this is equivalent to using the rescaled score \(f(X)=m(X)/(1-\rho)\). So if \(m(X)\) is already close to the right outcome scale, PPI can push it in the wrong direction.

PPI++

PPI++ is AIPW with empirical efficiency maximization over the one-score scaling class.

\[ \hat\theta_{\mathrm{PPI++}} = \hat\theta(\hat\lambda\,m). \]

The scale factor \(\lambda\) is chosen from the labeled data to make the final estimator more efficient.

In this one-score setting, PPI++ is asymptotically equivalent to linear calibration, even though the finite-sample estimators are not identical.

Empirical efficiency maximization

In the one-score case, this means choosing \(\lambda\) to minimize the estimated influence-function variance over the scaling class \(\{\lambda m(X)\}\). A compact summary is:

\[ \hat\lambda \in \arg\min_{\lambda}\,\hat\sigma^2(\lambda m). \]

One AIPW template, several score choices

The outer estimator stays the same. What changes is the score that goes into it.

Standard AIPW is the raw-score baseline. PPI is an inefficient variant that drops the labeled-sample plug-in contribution. PPI++ is AIPW with empirical efficiency maximization over the scaling class.

Calibration

Why calibration helps

Ranking helps, but the scale matters too because we average the score.

Calibration improves the score

A score can rank units well but still be numerically off. If it is too compressed, too steep, or shifted, calibration makes it more faithful to the outcome scale.

Mean calibration keeps the estimator aligned

After calibration, the average score on labeled rows is aligned with the observed outcome level. That is why the plug-in view and the AIPW correction view agree.

Quality and safety move together

Calibration can improve efficiency by giving AIPW a better score, while the labeled residual correction continues to guard against remaining error.

Starting from a raw score \(m(X)\), calibration replaces it by a transformed score \(g(m(X))\). The ideal one-dimensional target is \(E_0[Y \mid m(X)]\): that is the best squared-error predictor available from the original score alone. So calibration is trying to make the score as predictive as possible without asking for new features or retraining the original model.

Why Mean Calibration Is Enough

Write the target mean as \(\theta_0 = E_0[Y]\). If a calibrated score satisfies the mean-calibration condition

\[ E_0[Y-f(X)] = 0. \]

then automatically

\[ E_0[f(X)] = E_0[Y] = \theta_0. \]

So the pooled plug-in estimator \(\frac{1}{n+N}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(X_i) + \sum_{j=1}^{N} f(\widetilde X_j)\right]\) is targeting the right mean by itself. The AIPW version keeps the extra labeled correction term \(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\{Y_i-f(X_i)\}\), which is centered at zero under mean calibration and acts as a safeguard in finite samples.

Schematic 3

From a mis-scaled score to a better estimator input

Calibration is not just cosmetic. It changes the score that the estimator averages.

Once the calibrated score has the right average relationship to observed outcomes on labeled data, the plug-in and AIPW views are describing the same estimator.

Flexible Learning

Why data-adaptive learning is allowed

You do not have to hand-build a tiny parametric score just to stay on safe inferential ground.

Use flexible scores when they help

The score \(f(X)\) can be learned with modern machine learning if that gives a better approximation to \(\mu_0(X)\). Better approximation usually means better efficiency.

The correction protects you

AIPW is robust because the labeled residual term corrects the prediction layer. That is why flexible outcome learning can be used without turning the estimator into a naive plug-in average.

Cross-fitting is the practical backup

If the labeled-sample fitting is very aggressive, split the labeled data into folds, fit the score on one fold, and apply the correction on another. That cross-fitting step avoids overusing the same labels.

For simpler fits, standard regularity conditions are often enough. For more aggressive machine learning, cross-fitting is the usual safeguard.

Takeaway

What this means in practice

Think of the estimator as a safe wrapper around a score you are trying to improve.

Practical takeaway

  • AIPW is the safe wrapper.
  • \(f(X)\) is the efficiency lever.
  • Calibration is one way to improve that lever.
  • PPI and PPI++ are specific choices inside this broader workflow.
